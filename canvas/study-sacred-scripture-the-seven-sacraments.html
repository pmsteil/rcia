The Seven Sacraments

According to the Catechism of the Catholic Church (CCC), a sacrament is "an efficacious sign of grace, instituted by Christ and entrusted to the Church, by which divine life is dispensed to us." (CCC 1131)

This definition highlights the following important points about sacraments:

  • They are signs, which means that they are visible/physical symbols of something invisible, namely, God's grace.
  • They are efficacious, which means that they actually produce what they signify. In other words, when we receive a sacrament, we truly receive God's grace.
  • They were instituted by Christ, meaning that they are not human inventions, but rather gifts from God.
  • They are entrusted to the Church, meaning that the Church is responsible for celebrating and administering the sacraments.
  • They dispense divine life to us, meaning that they help us to grow in our relationship with God and to become more like Christ.

The CCC goes on to explain that the sacraments are "necessary for salvation" (CCC 1129). The church does not teach that the seven sacraments are the only way to receive Salvation, but they are the ordinary ways that the Church dispenses God's Grace for all people and all time. However, it is important to note that the sacraments are not "magic spells". They only work if we receive them with faith and with a sincere desire to grow in our relationship with God.

Here are the seven sacraments in the Catholic Church:

  • Baptism
  • Confirmation
  • Holy Eucharist
  • Reconciliation
  • Anointing of the Sick
  • Holy Orders
  • Matrimony

Each sacrament has its own unique meaning and purpose, but they all share the common goal of helping us to grow in our relationship with God and to become more like Christ.

 

The seven sacraments ALL have Scriptural backing (even though they are not named as "sacraments" in the Bible (although Reconciliation is!)

Here are some examples:

Baptism:

  • Matthew 3:13-17: Jesus is baptized by John the Baptist.

  • John 3:3-5: Jesus tells Nicodemus that he must be "born again of water and the Spirit" in order to enter the kingdom of God.

  • Acts 2:38: Peter tells the crowd on the day of Pentecost when they were "cut to the heart" - what they are to do - he tells them to a) Repent, b) be Baptized for the forgiveness of sins and that they will receive the gift of the Holy Spirit. That is what the Church says happens in the Sacrament of Baptism.  We do Baptism because Jesus tells us to.

    • Therefore let the whole house of Israel know for certain that God has made him both Lord and Messiah, this Jesus whom you crucified.” 37 Now when they heard this, they were cut to the heart, and they asked Peter and the other apostles, “What are we to do, my brothers?” 38 Peter [said] to them, “Repent and be baptized,[g] every one of you, in the name of Jesus Christ for the forgiveness of your sins; and you will receive the gift of the holy Spirit. 39 For the promise is made to you and to your children and to all those far off, whomever the Lord our God will call.” 40 He testified with many other arguments, and was exhorting them, “Save yourselves from this corrupt generation.” 41 Those who accepted his message were baptized, and about three thousand persons were added that day.

Confirmation:

  • Acts 1:8: (Jesus speaking) But you will receive power when the holy Spirit comes upon you, and you will be my witnesses in Jerusalem, throughout Judea and Samaria, and to the ends of the earth.”
  • Acts 2:1-17 describes the coming of the Holy Spirit on Pentecost, which is a significant event in the history of the Church. It is the day when the Holy Spirit was given to the disciples and to all believers, and it is the day when the Church was officially born.

  • Acts 8:14-17: When the apostles in Jerusalem hear that Samaria has accepted the word of God, they send Peter and John to them. When Peter and John arrive, they pray for the Samaritans to receive the Holy Spirit.

  • We do Baptism because Jesus sent the Holy Spirit to confirm their Faith and send them out to all the nations!

Eucharist:

  • John 6 is the chapter in which Jesus gives the Bread of Life discourse. In this discourse, Jesus teaches that he is the bread of life and that those who eat his flesh and drink his blood will have eternal life.

    This discourse is significant because it foreshadows the Eucharist, which is the sacrament in which we receive Jesus' body and blood. It also teaches us that Jesus is the source of all life and that we need him to sustain us on our spiritual journey.

  • Matthew 26:26-28: Jesus institutes the Eucharist at the Last Supper. He tells his disciples to take and eat the bread, saying, "This is my body, which is given for you. Do this in remembrance of me." He then tells them to take and drink the cup, saying, "This is my blood of the covenant, which is poured out for many for the forgiveness of sins. Do this, whenever you drink it, in remembrance of me."

  • Mark 14:22-24: Mark's account of the Last Supper is similar to Matthew's.

  • Luke 22:19-20: Luke's account of the Last Supper is also similar to Matthew's.

  • 1 Corinthians 11:23-26: Paul reminds the Corinthians of the institution of the Eucharist and tells them to celebrate it in a worthy manner:  27 Therefore whoever eats the bread or drinks the cup of the Lord unworthily will have to answer for the body and blood of the Lord.[l] 28 A person should examine himself,[m] and so eat the bread and drink the cup. 29 For anyone who eats and drinks without discerning the body, eats and drinks judgment[n] on himself. 30 That is why many among you are ill and infirm, and a considerable number are dying. 31 If we discerned ourselves, we would not be under judgment; 32 but since we are judged by [the] Lord, we are being disciplined so that we may not be condemned along with the world.

  • We celebrate the Sacrament of the Eucharist because Jesus told us to emphatically - see John 6 especially and Paul and the entire Church for the first 1500 years of Christianity did this.

Reconciliation:

  • Matthew 16:19: Jesus says to Peter, "I will give you the keys of the kingdom of heaven, and whatever you bind on earth shall be bound in heaven, and whatever you loose on earth shall be loosed in heaven."
  • Matthew 18:18: Jesus says to the disciples, "Truly, I say to you, whatever you bind on earth shall be bound in heaven, and whatever you loose on earth shall be loosed in heaven."
  • John 20:21-23: Jesus says to the disciples, "Receive the Holy Spirit. If you forgive the sins of any, they are forgiven them; if you retain the sins of any, they are retained."

Anointing of the Sick:

  • Mark 6:12-13: Jesus sends out his twelve disciples and gives them authority to cast out impure spirits and to heal every disease and sickness.

  • James 5:14-16: James tells the sick to call for the elders of the church and to have them pray over them, anointing them with oil in the name of the Lord.

Holy Orders:

  • Acts 1:15-26: When Judas commits suicide, the eleven apostles gather to choose a replacement. They pray and cast lots, and Matthias is chosen.

  • 1 Timothy 3:1-13: Paul gives Timothy a list of qualifications for elders and deacons.

  • Titus 1:5-9: Paul gives Titus a list of qualifications for elders.

Matrimony:

  • Genesis 2:18-25: God creates Eve to be a helper for Adam and they are married.

  • Matthew 19:3-9: When the Pharisees ask Jesus whether it is lawful to divorce a wife for any reason, he quotes Genesis 2:24 and says, "Therefore a man shall leave his father and mother and hold fast to his wife, and the two shall become one flesh. So they are no longer two, but one flesh. What therefore God has joined together, let not man put asunder."

  • Ephesians 5:21-33: Paul compares the relationship between husband and wife to the relationship between Christ and the church. He tells husbands to love their wives as Christ loved the church and gave himself up for her. He tells wives to submit to their husbands as the church submits to Christ.
